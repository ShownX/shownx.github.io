About

I work on co-designing lifelong learning and adversarial post-training so foundation models keep improving themselves while staying aligned in production.

I am a Tech Lead and Senior Applied Scientist at AWS AI Labs. The questions that drive me are practical: how do you update a deployed multi-modal model fast enough to keep up with attackers without losing what it already knew? How do you align a model continuously, not as a one-shot post-training step? How much of that loop can a multi-agent system run on its own?

Most of my work moves between two layers — the systems I ship and the research that backs them. The self-evolving agent that triages threats, runs red-teaming, and patches models autonomously informs my research on continual on-policy distillation. The continual-learning benchmark I designed informs the next iteration of the agent. I think this is the right loop for an industry scientist: research grounded in real failure modes, systems grounded in defensible ideas.

Before AWS I did my Ph.D. at the University of Houston with Professor Ioannis A. Kakadiaris (2014–2019), working on face recognition, 3D reconstruction, and adversarial robustness. I have published 30+ papers at CVPR, ICCV, ECCV, and NeurIPS (1,600+ citations, h-index 10, 2 orals, 1 best paper). I review regularly for the top CV and ML venues.

Prospective interns: if you are a Ph.D. student interested in agentic systems, alignment, or lifelong learning of foundation models, email me your CV and a short research statement.

Currently working on

  • Continual on-policy distillation from experts — letting a single model absorb capabilities from many specialists without forgetting, while routing inputs to the right expertise at inference.
  • More natural continual learning for VLMs — moving past the disjoint-task assumption that dominates the literature, with a benchmark that controls inter-task overlap.
  • Hybrid attention/recurrent architectures for long-context agentic workloads where attention is the latency bottleneck.
  • Adversarial post-training for production cadence — what the RLHF playbook gets wrong when the threat model is alive and adapting.

Research

My research and the systems I ship are one loop. Below are the systems I have led at AWS (translated to ideas, since most of the implementations are not public), the open questions driving my current work, and the trajectory that connects them.

Systems

Self-Evolving Agent — production, AWS

A hierarchical multi-agent system that runs the threat-to-patch loop autonomously. Three top-level agents — Triage, Red Team, Model Patch & Handoff — coordinate specialized subagents that detect threats from tickets and logs, discover and validate attacks, and apply continual fine-tuning with automated benchmarking. Compresses launch cadence by ~75%. The interesting part is the design pattern: separating threat discovery from defense generation lets each side improve without destabilizing the other.

Multi-Agent Research Harness — production tooling

A coordination layer for concurrent autonomous agents working on the same codebase: isolated sub-agent contexts, Kanban-style task claims with stale-detection, one git worktree per session, skill-based knowledge injection, and independent verification gates. Outperforms single-session agents on complex tasks at ~6× lower cost and runs 200+ autonomous experiments per sprint. The principle: agents fail more by stepping on each other than by being individually weak.

Co-Scientist — internal research agent

An agent for autonomous scientific workflows: deep literature review, hypothesis generation and critique, experiment planning with explicit baselines, automated execution and tracking, and technical-report drafting with citations. Compresses literature surveys from 1–2 weeks to 1–2 days and full research iteration from ~3 months to ~1 month. The lesson: forcing the agent to write a baseline plan before any code runs is what makes its experiments interpretable.

Super-Alignment Pipeline — production alignment

End-to-end alignment for multi-modal foundation models: multi-modal red-teaming, distributed LLM-as-judge evaluation, and adversarial post-training with preference optimization. Reduced attack success rate by an order of magnitude across text and vision modalities while preserving task quality. The thing the literature underweights: alignment in production is a moving target. Static reward models decay against adapting attackers, which is why I treat alignment as a continual learning problem.

Digital Identity Defense — Amazon Rekognition

Production defense against presentation attacks, deepfakes, and injection attacks for billion-level annual identity checks. Designed a proactive challenge-response paradigm and shipped multiple model updates per year tracking new threat vectors. The technical risk: keeping false positive rates flat for legitimate users while attackers shift from physical spoofs to GenAI deepfakes — a continuous-shift problem that motivated the lifelong-learning research direction.

GPU-Accelerated FHE Inference — privacy-preserving AI

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (CKKS, BFV) on GPU with ~45× speedup at zero accuracy loss, enabling encrypted inference and similarity search for regulated markets. The contribution most academic FHE work skips: making the polynomial arithmetic memory-bandwidth efficient on real GPUs, not just asymptotically faster.

Open Questions

The questions I do not yet have good answers to, and that drive my current research:

  • How do you measure whether a continual-learning update preserves prior capabilities per skill, not just on aggregate benchmarks? Aggregate accuracy hides catastrophic gaps.
  • Can on-policy distillation merge expert models trained on overlapping tasks without a router penalty? Most current methods either keep all experts (cost) or pick one (forgetting).
  • What does an alignment objective look like when the threat model is itself learning? RLHF assumes a static preference distribution; production trust-and-safety violates that assumption every day.
  • Where is the right boundary between attention and recurrence in a long-context VLM? My current bet (~25% recurrent layers) was empirical. The principled answer is unclear.
  • How much of the ML research loop can a multi-agent system actually drive end-to-end before the human becomes the bottleneck for taste, not labor?

Research Arc

A coherent thread across a decade — each stage exposed the bottleneck that motivated the next.

  • 2014–2019, Ph.D.: 3D face reconstruction → face recognition under occlusion. Learned that robustness was the binding constraint, not raw accuracy.
  • 2019–2021: Domain adaptation (d-SNE, oral CVPR 2019), liveness and anti-spoofing. Took the robustness lesson into deployable systems.
  • 2021–2023: Deepfake detection, multi-modal trust. Discovered that defenses decay because attackers move; static models are not enough.
  • 2023–2025: Adversarial post-training, super-alignment, agentic systems for the threat-to-patch loop. Built the systems-level answer to decay.
  • 2025–present: Continual learning, on-policy distillation, hybrid VLMs. Building the model-level answer to decay so the systems can run faster.
